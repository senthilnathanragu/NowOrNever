## 1. READING COMPREHENSION (RC)

### Why It Matters

RC typically has **2 passages with 4−5 questions each** in TCS NQT — that's **8−10 questions** out of 25. This single topic can make or break your score.

### Types of RC Questions

**Type 1 — Main Idea / Central Theme**
These ask: "What is the passage primarily about?" or "What is the best title?"
→ Strategy: The answer is almost always in the **first or last paragraph.** Avoid answers that are too specific (one example from the passage) or too broad.

**Type 2 — Direct Fact Questions**
These ask something explicitly stated in the passage.
→ Strategy: Go back to the passage. The answer exists word-for-word or very close to it. Don't use your own knowledge.

**Type 3 — Inference Questions**
"The author implies..." or "It can be inferred that..."
→ Strategy: The answer must be logically derived from the passage — not assumed, not from outside knowledge. If the passage doesn't support it, it's wrong.

**Type 4 — Tone / Attitude Questions**
"The author's tone is..." or "The author's attitude toward X is..."
→ Common tone words and what they mean:

| Tone Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Laudatory | Praising |
| Scathing / Scornful | Harshly critical |
| Ambivalent | Having mixed feelings |
| Didactic | Instructive, preachy |
| Sarcastic | Mocking using irony |
| Nostalgic | Longing for the past |
| Objective / Neutral | Balanced, no bias |
| Cynical | Distrustful, pessimistic |
| Cautious / Tentative | Careful, uncertain |
| Euphoric | Intensely happy |

**Type 5 — Vocabulary in Context**
"As used in line X, the word '___' most closely means..."
→ Strategy: Go back to that exact sentence. Substitute each option in place of the word and pick the one that makes the most sense in that context. Don't pick the most common meaning of the word blindly.

**Type 6 — True / False / Cannot Be Determined**
→ Strategy: "Cannot be determined" is the answer when the passage neither confirms nor denies a statement. Students often choose T or F when the passage is simply silent on the matter.

### RC Speed Strategy

The two methods TCS students use are:

**Method 1 — Questions First:**
Read the questions before the passage, then skim the passage looking for those specific answers. Works best when questions are direct/factual. Saves time.

**Method 2 — Passage First:**
Read the entire passage once at a good pace (not word by word), then answer questions. Works best when many questions are inference-based. Better for comprehension.

**Recommended:** Use Method 1 for TCS NQT since the questions are mostly direct and factual.

### RC Practice Passage

**Passage:**
The ocean covers more than 70% of the Earth's surface and plays a crucial role in regulating the planet's climate. Ocean currents act as a global conveyor belt, transporting warm water from the equator toward the poles and cold water back toward the tropics. This system of circulation helps distribute heat around the planet, making many coastal regions warmer or cooler than they would otherwise be. Scientists have observed that rising global temperatures are causing disruptions to this conveyor belt system, potentially leading to more extreme weather events worldwide. The oceans also absorb significant amounts of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, slowing the pace of climate change — but this absorption is making ocean water more acidic, threatening marine life.

**Questions:**

**Q1.** What is the main idea of the passage?
- (A) The ocean is very large and deep
- (B) Global temperatures are rising rapidly
- (C) Oceans play a vital role in climate regulation but face growing threats
- (D) Marine life is endangered by pollution

**Answer: C** — The passage covers both the ocean's climate role AND the threats to it.

**Q2.** The term "global conveyor belt" in the passage refers to:
- (A) A shipping route across oceans
- (B) The system of ocean currents that distribute heat
- (C) Wind patterns over the ocean
- (D) The movement of marine life

**Answer: B** — Directly stated in lines 2−3.

**Q3.** Which of the following can be inferred from the passage?
- (A) Ocean acidification will completely destroy all marine life by 2050
- (B) Warm water is denser than cold water
- (C) Without ocean currents, coastal regions would experience more extreme temperatures
- (D) Scientists have found a way to stop climate change

**Answer: C** — The passage says currents make coastal regions "warmer or cooler than they would otherwise be," implying without them, temperatures would be more extreme.

**Q4.** The author's tone in the passage is best described as:
- (A) Alarming and panicked
- (B) Informative and cautionary
- (C) Celebratory
- (D) Sarcastic

**Answer: B** — The author presents facts and warns of potential consequences without being extreme.

## 2. FILL IN THE BLANKS

### Single Blank

**Strategy:**
- Read the full sentence and identify the tone (positive/negative)
- Look for contrast words: *although, but, however, despite, yet* → answer will contrast the given idea
- Look for agreement words: *therefore, thus, furthermore, similarly* → answer will continue the same idea
- Eliminate grammatically wrong options first

**Key Connectors to Remember:**

| Contrast Connectors | Agreement Connectors |
|---|---|
| Although, Though | Therefore, Thus |
| But, However | Hence, Consequently |
| Despite, In spite of | Furthermore, Moreover |
| Yet, Nevertheless | Similarly, Likewise |
| Whereas, While | In addition, Also |

### Practice Problems — Single Blank

**Q1.** Despite being _______, the professor's lecture held the students' attention throughout.
(A) engaging (B) monotonous (C) humorous (D) brief

**Answer: B** — "Despite" signals contrast. The lecture held attention (positive), so the blank must be negative → monotonous.

**Q2.** The scientist's _______ approach to the experiment ensured that every variable was carefully controlled.
(A) hasty (B) reckless (C) meticulous (D) casual

**Answer: C** — "Carefully controlled" tells us the approach was thorough and precise → meticulous.

**Q3.** The government's new policy was met with _______ from opposition leaders who called it unconstitutional.
(A) applause (B) indifference (C) censure (D) support

**Answer: C** — "Called it unconstitutional" shows disapproval → censure (official criticism).

### Double Blank

**Strategy:**
- Eliminate any option where even one word is wrong
- Usually both blanks are thematically connected (both positive or both negative or contrasting)
- Fill the easier blank first, then verify the second

### Practice Problems — Double Blank

**Q1.** The _______ nature of his arguments made it difficult to _______ his points during the debate.
(A) lucid ... misunderstand (B) convoluted ... comprehend (C) simple ... explain (D) logical ... counter

**Answer: B** — Convoluted (complicated) arguments → difficult to comprehend (understand). Logically consistent.

**Q2.** Though she appeared _______ on the surface, her writing revealed a deeply _______ mind.
(A) calm ... troubled (B) simple ... complex (C) arrogant ... humble (D) cheerful ... cheerful

**Answer: B** — "Though" signals contrast. Simple appearance vs complex inner mind.

## 3. SENTENCE CORRECTION / ERROR DETECTION

### The 6 Most Tested Grammar Rules in TCS

### Rule 1 — Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb must agree with its subject in number, not with words that come between them.

❌ "The quality of the mangoes are poor." → "The quality" is singular
✅ "The quality of the mangoes **is** poor."

**Tricky cases:**
- "Each / Every / Either / Neither / None / Anyone / Nobody" → always **singular verb**
- "Two and two makes four" → amounts, calculations → singular
- "The jury has reached its verdict" → collective nouns → usually singular in American English
- "Neither the students nor the teacher **was** present" → verb agrees with nearest subject

**Practice:**
"Neither of the answers _______ correct." → **is** (Neither = singular)
"The committee _______ divided on the issue." → **was** (committee = singular)

### Rule 2 — Tense Consistency

All verbs in a sentence or paragraph must follow a logical tense sequence.

❌ "She went to the market and buys vegetables."
✅ "She went to the market and **bought** vegetables."

**Time Sequence Rules:**
- Past Perfect (had + V3) is used for the earlier of two past events
- "By the time he arrived, she **had already left**." → leaving happened first → past perfect

**Practice:**
"When I reached the station, the train _______ (leave)." → **had already left**

### Rule 3 — Articles (a, an, the)

**"A" vs "An":**
- Based on SOUND, not spelling
- An hour ✅ (hour sounds like "our" — vowel sound)
- A university ✅ (university sounds like "you" — consonant sound)
- An MBA ✅ (M sounds like "em" — vowel sound)
- A one-time offer ✅ (one sounds like "won" — consonant sound)

**"The" Usage:**
- Superlatives: the best, the tallest
- Unique things: the sun, the moon, the sky
- Second mention: "I saw a dog. **The** dog was barking."
- Specific reference: "**The** book you gave me is excellent."

**No Article:**
- Before languages: She speaks **Hindi** (not the Hindi)
- Before meals: **Dinner** is ready
- Before proper nouns: **India** is great (not the India — exception: the USA, the UK, the Philippines)

### Rule 4 — Pronouns

**Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement:**
❌ "Each student should submit their assignment." (traditional rule)
✅ "Each student should submit **his or her** assignment."
(Note: TCS may accept "their" as gender-neutral in modern usage)

**Pronoun Case:**
- Subject pronouns: I, he, she, we, they, who
- Object pronouns: me, him, her, us, them, whom

❌ "Between you and I, this is wrong."
✅ "Between you and **me**, this is wrong." (after preposition → object pronoun)

**Who vs Whom:**
- Who = subject (doing the action)
- Whom = object (receiving the action)
- "**Who** called?" vs "**Whom** did you call?"
- Trick: Replace with he/she → use "who" | Replace with him/her → use "whom"

### Rule 5 — Modifiers (Dangling & Misplaced)

A modifier must be placed next to the word it modifies.

❌ "Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful." (Trees can't walk)
✅ "Walking down the street, **I** found the trees beautiful."

❌ "She almost drove her children to school every day." (Almost never did it?)
✅ "She drove her children to school **almost** every day."

### Rule 6 — Prepositions (Commonly Tested Pairs)

| Correct Usage | Wrong |
|---|---|
| Agree **with** a person | Agree to a person ❌ |
| Agree **to** a proposal | Agree with a proposal ❌ |
| Afraid **of** | Afraid from ❌ |
| Accused **of** | Accused for ❌ |
| Angry **with** a person | Angry on a person ❌ |
| Angry **at** a thing | Angry with a thing ❌ |
| Consists **of** | Consists with ❌ |
| Differ **from** (in nature) | — |
| Differ **with** (in opinion) | — |
| Eligible **for** | Eligible of ❌ |
| Good **at** (a skill) | Good in ❌ |
| Married **to** | Married with ❌ |
| Inferior **to** | Inferior than ❌ |
| Die **of** (a disease) | Die from (a disease — British: acceptable) |
| Jealous **of** | Jealous from ❌ |

### Practice Problems — Error Detection

**Q1.** Find the error: "She is one of those students who is always on time."
- "One of those students" → plural antecedent (students) → **"who are always on time"** ✅

**Q2.** Find the error: "He is more smarter than his brother."
- Double comparative → ❌ "more smarter"
- ✅ "He is **smarter** than his brother."

**Q3.** Find the error: "Neither the principal nor the teachers was present."
- "Neither...nor" → verb agrees with nearest subject (teachers = plural)
- ✅ "Neither the principal nor the teachers **were** present."

**Q4.** Find the error: "The committee have decided to postpone the meeting."
- Committee = collective noun = singular verb
- ✅ "The committee **has** decided."

## 4. PARA JUMBLES (Sentence Rearrangement)

### Step-by-Step Strategy

**Step 1 — Find the Opening Sentence**
The first sentence of a paragraph:
- Introduces a topic, person, or concept for the first time (no pronoun referring back to something)
- Never starts with "However," "But," "Therefore," "This," "These," "Such"
- Usually contains a broad general statement

**Step 2 — Find the Closing Sentence**
The last sentence:
- Contains a conclusion, result, or summary
- May use words like "thus," "therefore," "finally," "in conclusion"
- Doesn't introduce any new idea that needs continuation

**Step 3 — Find Mandatory Pairs (Link Sentences)**
Look for:
- Pronoun references: If sentence B says "He did this," sentence A must introduce "he"
- Demonstratives: "This discovery," "That idea," "These results" → the previous sentence must have mentioned that discovery/idea/results
- Connectors: "However," "Moreover," "Therefore," "Consequently" → these connect to the previous idea
- Examples: "For instance," "For example" → must follow a general statement

### Para Jumble — Worked Example

Jumble these sentences:
- **P:** This led to widespread criticism from environmental groups worldwide.
- **Q:** The company announced plans to build a new factory in the protected forest area.
- **R:** Ultimately, the government intervened and cancelled the project.
- **S:** Despite public outcry, the company initially refused to reconsider its decision.

**Analysis:**
- Q = Opening sentence (introduces company and plan for first time, no pronoun referring back)
- P = After Q (Q announces plan → P shows reaction "this led to criticism")
- S = After P (despite the criticism/outcry, company refused → S)
- R = Closing (Ultimately = conclusion signal → government cancelled)

**Order: Q → P → S → R**

### More Para Jumble Practice

**Sentences:**
- **A:** For centuries, humans have been fascinated by the stars.
- **B:** With modern telescopes, we can now see galaxies billions of light-years away.
- **C:** Early civilizations used them for navigation and agriculture.
- **D:** This curiosity has driven enormous advances in astronomical technology.

**Analysis:**
- A = Opening (introduces general topic, no pronouns referring back)
- C = After A ("them" refers to "the stars" from A)
- D = After C ("this curiosity" refers to fascination mentioned in A, driven by what C said)
- B = Closing (Result of advances → modern telescopes, "now" signals current state)

**Order: A → C → D → B**

## 8. CLOZE TEST

A Cloze Test is a paragraph with multiple blanks. Each blank must be filled based on context, tone, and grammar.

### Strategy

1. Read the entire passage once without filling blanks — understand the main idea and tone
2. Fill the easiest blanks first (where you're confident)
3. Use the filled blanks to give context clues for the harder blanks
4. Check subject-verb agreement and tense consistency after filling

### Practice Cloze Test

**Fill in the blanks:**

"Technology has (1)_______ the way we communicate. In the past, people (2)_______ letters that took days or weeks to arrive. Today, a message can (3)_______ the other side of the world in seconds. However, despite this (4)_______ in communication speed, some argue that the quality of our connections has (5)_______ , with face-to-face interactions becoming increasingly rare."

**Options:**
1. (A) destroyed (B) transformed (C) maintained (D) invented
2. (A) send (B) sent (C) sends (D) have sent
3. (A) arrive (B) reach (C) land (D) travel
4. (A) problem (B) failure (C) advancement (D) return
5. (A) improved (B) increased (C) deteriorated (D) expanded

**Answers:**
1. **B** (transformed — technology changed communication)
2. **B** (sent — past tense, "In the past")
3. **B** (reach — idiomatically, "reach the other side")
4. **C** (advancement — positive development in speed)
5. **C** (deteriorated — "however" signals contrast; speed is better but quality is worse)

## 9. ACTIVE & PASSIVE VOICE

### Rules

**Active:** Subject → Verb → Object
**Passive:** Object of active becomes Subject + "is/are/was/were/been" + Past Participle + "by" + Subject

| Tense | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| Simple Present | She writes a letter | A letter is written by her |
| Simple Past | She wrote a letter | A letter was written by her |
| Present Continuous | She is writing a letter | A letter is being written by her |
| Past Continuous | She was writing a letter | A letter was being written by her |
| Present Perfect | She has written a letter | A letter has been written by her |
| Past Perfect | She had written a letter | A letter had been written by her |
| Simple Future | She will write a letter | A letter will be written by her |
| Future Perfect | She will have written | A letter will have been written by her |

**Object Pronouns in Passive:**
I → me → by me | He → him → by him | She → her → by her
We → us → by us | They → them → by them | You → you → by you

### Practice

**Q1.** Active: "They are building a new bridge."
Passive: "A new bridge **is being built** by them." ✅

**Q2.** Active: "The manager had approved the proposal."
Passive: "The proposal **had been approved** by the manager." ✅

**Q3.** Active: "Who wrote this letter?"
Passive: "By whom **was** this letter **written**?" ✅

## 10. DIRECT & INDIRECT SPEECH

### Reporting Verb Changes (Tense Backshift)

| Direct Speech Tense | Indirect Speech Tense |
|---|---|
| Simple Present | Simple Past |
| Present Continuous | Past Continuous |
| Present Perfect | Past Perfect |
| Simple Past | Past Perfect |
| Will | Would |
| Can | Could |
| May | Might |
| Shall | Should |

### Pronoun & Time Word Changes

| Direct | Indirect |
|---|---|
| I | he/she |
| we | they |
| you | I / he / she / they (context) |
| now | then |
| today | that day |
| yesterday | the previous day / the day before |
| tomorrow | the next day / the following day |
| here | there |
| this | that |
| these | those |
| last night | the previous night |
| ago | before |

### Types of Sentences in Indirect Speech

**Statements:** Use "that"
"She said, 'I am tired.'" → She said **that** she **was** tired.

**Questions (Yes/No):** Use "if" or "whether"
"He asked, 'Are you coming?'" → He asked **if** I **was** coming.

**Questions (Wh-):** Use the wh-word directly
"She asked, 'Where do you live?'" → She asked **where** I **lived.**

**Commands:** Use "to + infinitive" (told/asked/ordered + to)
"He said, 'Open the door.'" → He **told** me **to open** the door.

**Requests:** Use "requested + to"
"She said, 'Please help me.'" → She **requested** me **to help** her.

### Practice

**Q1.** Direct: "The teacher said, 'The earth revolves around the sun.'"
Indirect: The teacher said **that the earth revolves around the sun.** ✅
(Universal truths do NOT change tense)

**Q2.** Direct: "He said, 'I will meet you tomorrow.'"
Indirect: He said **that he would meet me the next day.** ✅

**Q3.** Direct: "She asked, 'Have you finished your work?'"
Indirect: She asked **whether I had finished my work.** ✅

## 🎯 EXAM-DAY STRATEGY FOR VERBAL

**Attempt Order:**
1. **RC first** — highest weightage, do it while your mind is fresh (attempt both passages back to back)
2. **Grammar-based questions next** — error detection, fill in the blanks (rules either apply or they don't — fast answers)
3. **Para Jumbles** — do these after RC and grammar, they require logical thinking
4. **Vocabulary questions last** — synonyms, antonyms, idioms (either you know or you don't — don't spend more than 20 seconds)

**The 3 Don'ts:**
- Don't translate English into your mother tongue in your head — it slows you down. Practice thinking in English.
- Don't leave RC for last — it has the most marks and you'll be rushing.
- Don't overthink grammar — your first instinct after learning the rules is usually right.

**Daily Practice Plan for Verbal:**
- 10 new vocabulary words every single day — write them, use them in a sentence
- Read one English article per day (The Hindu editorial is perfect for TCS level)
- Solve 5 RC passages per week
- Do 10 fill-in-the-blank/error correction questions daily
